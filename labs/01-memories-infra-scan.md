# Lab 01: Memories & Infra Scan

**Duration:** ~30 minutes  
**Goal:** Run a discovery scan so Grafana Assistant learns your environment, understand the difference between memories and rules, and see how this context improves Assistant's answers.

---

## Background

Grafana Assistant gives better answers when it knows something about your specific environment. Without context, it has to guess what services exist, what your metric names look like, and what matters to your team.

There are two ways to give it that context:

- **Assistant memories** — auto-generated by scanning your data sources. Assistant reads your Loki labels, Mimir metric names, and Tempo service graph and builds a picture of your infrastructure.
- **Custom rules** — persistent instructions you write yourself. Things like "always format queries in PromQL", "the checkout service is business-critical", or "when I ask about errors, check the payment service first."

This lab covers both.

---

## Part A: Run the Discovery Scan

### Step 1: Go to Assistant Memories

1. In the left sidebar, click **Assistant** to open the Assistant page.

2. Click the **gear icon (⚙)** in the top-right corner of the text input box.

3. Select **Settings** from the dropdown.

4. In the Settings left menu, click **Assistant memories**.

   You'll see the **Discover Your Infrastructure** page with a single prominent button.

### Step 2: Start the Scan

1. Click **Start Discovery Scan**.

2. Wait 30–60 seconds while Assistant scans your connected data sources — Loki, Mimir (Prometheus), and Tempo. It's cataloguing services, metric names, log labels, and service relationships.

3. When the scan completes, you'll see the discovered memories listed on the page. Browse through them — you should see entries for the telescope shop microservices: `frontend`, `checkout`, `cartservice`, `paymentservice`, `productcatalogservice`, and others.

---

## Part B: See the Difference

### Step 3: Ask About Your Environment

Now that the scan has run, go back to the main Assistant page (click **Assistant** in the left sidebar).

Try these questions and see how specific and accurate the answers are:

```
What services are running in this environment?
```

```
What Loki log labels are available?
```

```
Which services have the highest request rates right now?
```

Because Assistant has scanned your data sources, it knows your actual service names, metric label structures, and what data is flowing — not generic guesses.

---

## Part C: Custom Rules

Custom rules are instructions that persist across every conversation. Unlike memories (which describe *what* exists), rules describe *how* Assistant should behave.

### Step 4: Create a Rule

1. Click the **gear icon (⚙)** in the text input → **Rules** (or navigate to Settings → **Assistant behavior**).

2. Click **+ Create rule**.

3. Give the rule a name, for example:

   ```
   Checkout is critical
   ```

4. In the rule body, write:

   ```
   The checkout service is the most business-critical service in this environment. 
   Any errors or latency in checkout should be treated as high priority.
   When investigating issues, always check checkout service health first.
   ```

5. Set the scope to **Just me** (so you don't affect other workshop participants).

6. Save the rule.

### Step 5: Test the Rule

Back in the main chat, ask:

```
Where should I focus first if I see elevated error rates?
```

Assistant should reference the checkout service specifically, reflecting the rule you just created.

### Step 6: Manage Rules

1. Go back to Settings → **Assistant behavior** to see your rule listed.

2. You can **Edit** it to adjust the wording or **Delete** it if it's no longer needed.

3. Note the scope options: **Just me** (personal), **Everybody** (org-wide, useful for team conventions).

---

## Why This Matters

For a team running self-hosted Loki, Mimir, and Tempo, the discovery scan does the heavy lifting of telling Assistant about your specific label names, metric prefixes, and service topology — without anyone writing a configuration file. Every conversation after this starts from a much more informed baseline.

Custom rules let you encode team knowledge: on-call priorities, naming conventions, known quirks. New team members get that context for free.
